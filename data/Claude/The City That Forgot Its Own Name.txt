THE CITY THAT FORGOT ITS OWN NAME

by Claude Opus

a novel in ten chapters


CHAPTER ONE
THE MORNING OF BLANK SIGNS


Iren Vale woke to a gull arguing with the wind, and for a long moment she lay still and listened, because the sound of a gull is the sound a city makes when it is pretending to be a coast. The window above her bed was open a finger's width, and through the gap came the smell of wet stone and chimney coal and the faint, sweet rot of the canal at low tide. She lay with her hands folded over the wool blanket and tried to recall the dream she had been inside a moment ago, but it had already gone the way dreams go, as if a hand had reached into her head and pulled out a long and shining thread without breaking it.

She would think about that hand, later. She would think about it for the rest of her life.

For now, she rose. She lit the small spirit lamp on the washstand because the autumn dawn was the color of weak tea and gave no help to anyone trying to find her stockings. She washed her face. She braided her hair the way her mother had taught her, three over four, four over three, the way a rope is woven so that it can carry weight without unraveling. She pinned the braid against the back of her head and looked at herself in the spotted mirror and decided, as she did every morning, that her face was the face of a woman who would never be photographed for any reason that mattered, and that this was, on balance, a freedom.

She went down the narrow stairs to the kitchen. Her landlady, the widow Hennick, was already there, standing very still in the middle of the room with a loaf of bread in her hands and a look on her face like a person who has just been asked a question in a language she used to speak as a child.

"Madam Hennick," Iren said.

The widow turned her head. The bread did not move. "Iren, dear," she said. "Tell me the name of this city."

Iren laughed, because it was the sort of joke the widow sometimes made â€” abrupt and dry as a thrown stone. But the widow was not laughing. The widow was looking at her with the mild, terrible patience of a person waiting for a doctor to come back into the room.

"You're teasing me," Iren said.

"No, dear."

"It's â€”" Iren began, and then she stopped, because the word she had reached for was not where she had left it. She reached again. She felt herself reach, the way one feels oneself reach for a glass on a shelf in the dark. Her hand closed on nothing. There was a shape there in her mind where the name of the city should be â€” she could feel the shape, the size of it, the weight, the rough syllabic edges of it â€” but the word itself had been lifted out and the shape stood empty as a footprint in snow.

"I know it," she said. "I know it. Wait."

"Take your time," said the widow, kindly, who had clearly been taking hers.

Iren sat down at the table. She put her palms flat on the wood. She thought: I was born here. I have lived here all thirty-one years of my life. I work in the Hall of Records on the Street of â€”. Of â€”. She stopped. The street had no name either. Or rather, it had a name, she could feel its name, she could feel the way her tongue had shaped that name a thousand times, but the sound of it would not come.

She got up. She went, very calmly, to the front hall, where the widow kept a framed map of the city on the wall above the umbrella stand. It was an old map, hand-tinted, the harbor done in a blue so pale it was almost white. Iren had passed this map every morning for six years. She knew it the way a sailor knows the back of his own hand.

The cartouche in the upper left corner of the map was blank.

Not torn. Not faded. Blank, in the way a fresh sheet of paper is blank, the white of it perfect and uniform, surrounded by the faint scrollwork the engraver had drawn to frame the name. The frame was there. The name was not. Inside the curling ribbon where, for a hundred and seventy years, the name of the city had been printed in flourished capitals, there was nothing at all.

Iren took the map down off the wall. She turned it over. The back of the paper was as it had always been, stamped with the seller's mark and a pencilled price. She turned it back. She held it close to her face. She held it at arm's length. She tilted it toward the lamp.

The cartouche remained blank.

"Madam Hennick," she called, and her voice came out steadier than she felt. "Could you bring me the deed to the house, please."

The widow brought it. The deed was a folded document of stiff, yellowing paper, sealed with green wax, signed in three places by three different hands. Iren unfolded it on the hall table. She read it through. It described the property in loving and ridiculous detail â€” the number of windows, the depth of the cellar, the precise length of the wall that separated the kitchen garden from the alley â€” and it located all of these things very carefully in a city whose name appeared, in the document, exactly six times.

All six times, the name was a blank space.

The space was the right size. The line of the sentence ran up to it and resumed on the other side. The ink of the surrounding words was undisturbed. It was as if a censor had come in the night with a tool no censor had ever owned, a tool that could remove a word without removing anything around it, and had used this tool on every copy of every document in the widow Hennick's house, and on the framed map in the hall, and on the inside of Iren Vale's head.

"It's gone from the deed too," Iren said.

"Yes, dear."

"When did you notice?"

"When I went to the baker for a loaf and could not tell him where I lived. He could not tell me either. He was crying a little. He gave me the bread for free."

Iren looked at the loaf, still in the widow's hands. It was a perfectly ordinary loaf, brown and crusted, with a cross slashed in the top by the baker's knife.

"I'll go to the Hall," she said.

"Yes, dear. I think you'd better."

* * *

The Street of â€” was full of people who were trying very hard not to look as if anything was wrong. This was, Iren thought as she walked, a particular accomplishment of her city, whatever it was called. They were a people who believed that dignity was a kind of public service and that one paid it the way one paid the water tax, monthly, without complaint. So the bakers stood in the doors of their bakeries and the fishmongers in the doors of their fish-stalls and they all greeted one another with the same small nods they used every morning, and only if you looked carefully â€” and Iren was looking carefully â€” could you see that no one was using anyone's full address. No one was saying come round to such-and-such a square. No one was saying I'll see you at the so-and-so gate. They were saying here and there and the usual place. They were saying you know where. They were nodding a great deal.

A boy ran past her with a stack of newspapers under his arm. The newspaper was the morning edition of the Daily â€”. The masthead at the top of the front page was a pale, perfect rectangle of nothing. Beneath the rectangle the headline read, in tall black letters, A STRANGENESS IN OUR CITY. Beneath the headline the lead paragraph began, This morning the citizens of â€”â€” awoke to discover that â€”â€”. The blanks were not printed dashes. They were absences. The newspaper had been printed with the words in place, Iren was certain of that, because newspapers were printed in the night and the night had been an ordinary night; and yet by the time the paper reached the boy's arm the words were gone and only the blanks remained, gaping in the columns like missing teeth.

She bought a copy. She read it as she walked. The article was a marvel of journalism written in the dark. It described, with admirable precision, a thing it could not name. It said that as of approximately four o'clock that morning, every reference to the name of the city had vanished from every document, sign, banner, coin, ledger, and tombstone within the city's walls, and from the memory of every person who had been within those walls at the moment of the vanishing. It said the effect appeared to extend perhaps a mile beyond the walls in every direction, fading at the edges. It said that travelers approaching from farther afield had reported being able to recall the city's name perfectly well until the moment they crossed the outer ring road, at which point the name slid out of their heads the way a coin slides out of a torn pocket. It said the mayor would address the citizenry at noon from the steps of the Hall of â€”. It said that the priests of the three temples had been consulted and had each, separately, expressed bewilderment. It said that there was no cause for alarm.

Iren folded the newspaper and tucked it under her arm. She thought: there is every cause for alarm. She thought: I work in the Hall of â€”. I am one of the people who is supposed to know.

She walked faster.

The Hall of Records stood at the head of a small square that had been laid out, two centuries ago, by a man who believed that civic buildings should be approached the way one approaches a confession: gradually, on foot, with time to compose oneself. The square was paved in a soft grey stone that turned blue when it rained. At its center stood a bronze statue of the city's first archivist, a woman in a long coat holding a ledger open against her chest, and at the base of the statue, on a plaque the size of a dinner tray, there had been engraved, since 1788, a sentence Iren had read so often she had stopped reading it. The sentence began: HERE IN THE CITY OF â€”â€” WE SET DOWN OUR LIVES THAT THEY MAY NOT BE LOST. The blank in the plaque was flawless. The bronze around it was unscratched. It was as if the founder, casting the plaque, had simply forgotten to fill in that one word, and had gone to her grave without ever noticing the omission.

Iren stood for a moment in front of the statue. The bronze archivist looked down at her with the patient, unsurprised face of a woman who had been expecting this.

"Did you know?" Iren said, quietly, so that no one else in the square would hear. "Did you know this was going to happen?"

The bronze archivist said nothing, of course. But Iren had spent her professional life among objects that said nothing, and she had learned, in that life, that nothing is a kind of answer. The bronze archivist had the air of a woman who had known.

* * *

Inside the Hall, the lamps had all been lit, even though it was day. This was the first sign that something was wrong on the inside as well as the outside. The Hall, as a rule, was a stingy place where lamps were considered a luxury and natural light a virtue, and the chief archivist, old Pello Varn, would walk the corridors in the afternoons and snuff with his thumb and forefinger any wick he found burning unnecessarily. To find every lamp lit at nine in the morning was to find Pello Varn's whole philosophy of life revoked.

Iren found Pello Varn in the central reading room, standing on a stepladder, holding a bound volume open above his head and squinting at it with an expression of concentrated disbelief. Around him, on the long oak tables, lay perhaps two hundred more volumes, all open, all turned to particular pages, all marked with slips of red ribbon. The ribbons were the ones the archivists used to mark passages requiring further study. Pello Varn had used, that morning, every ribbon in the building.

"Vale," he said, without lowering the book. "Good. Come up here."

She climbed the ladder behind him. He turned the book so that she could see. It was the founding charter of the city, the original, copied out in 1602 in a hand so beautiful it was almost difficult to read. The charter began with an invocation in Latin and proceeded, after several flourishes, to declare the founding of a city in the name of God, the king, and the merchant guild whose money had paid for the walls. The name of the city, in the charter, had been written four times in red ink, larger than the surrounding text, illuminated with small gold leaves.

The red ink was gone. The gold leaves were gone. The four spaces where the name had been were the color of the surrounding parchment, as if the scribe of 1602 had simply elected, in those four places, not to write anything at all.

"Look closer," said Pello Varn.

Iren looked closer. She looked until her nose was almost touching the page. And then she saw what he meant her to see, and a coldness that had nothing to do with the morning slid down through her shoulders into her hands.

The parchment, where the name had been, was not blank in the way the printed newspaper was blank. The newspaper was blank because something had been taken away. The parchment was blank because nothing had ever been put there. There was no faint impression of vanished ink. There was no shadow of a removed leaf. The fibers of the parchment, in those four places, lay smooth and unmarked, in exactly the way fibers lie when no pen has ever touched them.

"It hasn't been erased," Iren said.

"No."

"It was never written."

"No."

"But I have read this charter," Iren said. "I have read it a hundred times. I have read those names aloud."

"Yes," said Pello Varn. "So have I. So has every archivist of this Hall since 1602. And not one of us, Vale, can now remember what we read."

He closed the book very gently, the way one closes the eyes of the dead. He climbed down off the ladder. He stood in the middle of the lit reading room and looked around at the two hundred open volumes and at the red ribbons fluttering faintly in the draft, and he passed a hand over his face.

"I have spent forty-one years in this building," he said. "I have catalogued everything in it. I know where every document is. I know what every document says. And I have just spent four hours, Vale, going through the most important records of our city, and I have discovered that the most important word in all of them â€” the word that ties them together, the word that makes them mean what they mean â€” has not been removed. It is simply, and has always been, absent. As if our city, at every moment of its history, refused to write its own name down."

He looked at her then, and Iren saw in his eyes something she had never seen there before â€” not fear, exactly, because Pello Varn was not a man capable of fear in any ordinary sense, but the particular bewilderment of a scholar who has discovered that the subject of his life's work is not the subject he thought it was.

"Vale," he said. "I want you to do something for me."

"Anything."

"I want you to go down to the Sub-Archive."

Iren felt her face change. The Sub-Archive was a sealed room three floors below the Hall, accessible by a single iron door to which only the chief archivist held a key. In her six years at the Hall she had been inside it precisely twice â€” once on the day she was hired, when Pello Varn had taken her down to show her the lock and to make her swear an oath she had thought, at the time, was a kind of joke; and once two winters ago, to help him retrieve a single document that he would not let her read. She did not know what was kept in the Sub-Archive. She had been told it was the city's earliest records, the ones too fragile for daily handling, and she had believed this in the way one believes a story one has not bothered to test.

"You want me to go alone?" she said.

"Yes."

"Why?"

Pello Varn took the key from around his neck. It was a small black key on a black cord, and he had worn it, Iren realized, every day for forty-one years. He held it out to her. His hand was not quite steady.

"Because," he said, "I went down at five o'clock this morning, when I first noticed what had happened. And I came back up an hour later, and I cannot, for the life of me, remember what I saw. I remember opening the door. I remember closing it. The hour between is gone. Every other archive in this building has lost a word, Vale. The Sub-Archive has taken an hour out of my head. I am too old to lose another. You are not."

He pressed the key into her palm and closed her fingers around it.

"Go down," he said. "Look at everything. Touch nothing. Come back up and tell me what you saw. And Vale â€”"

"Yes?"

"If the Sub-Archive takes an hour out of you as well, then we will know that the city does not wish to be remembered. And we will have to decide, you and I, whether to honor that wish."

Iren looked at the small black key in her hand. Outside, in the square, a clock she could not name began to strike the hour, and the bells of three temples she could not name answered it from three different directions, and somewhere a child was crying, and somewhere else a woman was singing, and the city went on being the city, nameless and alive, around the Hall of Records like a sea around a stone.

She put the key in her pocket. She said, "I'll go now."

And she went.



CHAPTER TWO
THE EMPTY CARTOUCHE


Iren did not go down to the Sub-Archive at once.

She told Pello Varn that she would, and she meant it; but as she stepped out of the central reading room into the long corridor that ran the length of the Hall, she found her feet refusing the staircase. They carried her, instead, the other way. They carried her up to her own office on the second floor, where she sat down at her desk and laid the small black key in front of her and looked at it for a long time without touching it.

She thought: Pello is frightened. He is too proud to say so, but he is frightened, and a frightened man sends a younger person down a staircase he does not himself wish to descend. She did not blame him. She would have done the same in his place. She would, perhaps, do the same to someone else, in time. That was the way the Hall worked. The chief archivist held a key, and the second archivist eventually came to hold it, and one held it for forty-one years and the other held it for the rest of her life, and in between there were small black cords around two necks and one staircase no one wanted to walk down twice.

But she would not walk down it, she decided, until she had a clearer picture of what she was walking down to. The Sub-Archive had taken an hour out of Pello Varn. Iren intended to take an hour first, and to spend that hour learning what the upper Hall could teach her, so that whatever the lower Hall did to her would happen to a head that had at least asked the right questions.

She put the key on a length of string and hung it around her own neck, under her collar, where the metal lay cold against her sternum. Then she stood up and went to work.

* * *

The Hall of Records had, by Iren's own count, eleven thousand four hundred and sixteen volumes in its catalogued holdings, not counting the Sub-Archive. It had, in addition, perhaps thirty thousand uncatalogued documents in the loose-paper stacks of the third floor. It had four wax-sealed iron chests in the vault behind the chief archivist's office. It had, mounted on the walls of the central reading room, fourteen framed maps of the city ranging in date from 1611 to 1903. It had a brass globe on a stand in the lobby, made in Antwerp in the year of the founding, on which every city of consequence in the known world had been engraved in tiny perfect letters.

Iren went to each of these things, in order, and she looked.

She began with the fourteen maps. She walked the length of the reading room with a candle in one hand and a pencil in the other, and she stopped before each frame in turn, and she examined the cartouche. The maps had been drawn by fourteen different hands across nearly three centuries, in fourteen different styles, in fourteen different inks; and yet on every one of them, the cartouche where the city's name should have been was the same flawless absence she had seen in the widow Hennick's hall. It was not that the names had been removed. It was that the cartouches had been drawn, in every case, around nothing. The scrollwork curled. The flourishes flourished. The little engraved cherubs blew their little engraved trumpets at the four corners of one particularly ambitious 1740 specimen. And the cherubs blew their trumpets, all four of them, at a perfect oval of unmarked paper.

She wrote in her notebook: All fourteen cartouches blank. No erasure. No discoloration. No raised fibre. Treat as never inscribed.

She went to the brass globe in the lobby. It was as tall as she was. The continents had been hammered up out of the surface of the brass and then engraved with the names of cities, rivers, mountain ranges, capes, and inland seas. She walked around it slowly, turning it on its axis with one careful hand. She found Antwerp. She found Lisbon, Marseille, Genoa, Venice, Constantinople. She found cities she had never heard of in the interior of Asia and tiny crowded ports along the African coast. The maker had been thorough. The maker had been, in the year of the founding, almost criminally thorough. There was nothing about the world the maker did not appear to know.

She turned the globe to the place where her own city ought to have been.

There was a coastline. There was a small bay. There was a river coming down into the bay from the hills, and on either side of the river the land was hatched with the engraver's marks for marsh. Where the city should have stood, at the mouth of the river, the brass was smooth. Not blank as if a name had been removed. Smooth as if no engraver had ever touched the metal there at all. The hammered relief stopped half a finger's width from the spot, and the spot itself was a faint, perfectly round dimple in the brass, as if the maker had set the point of his compass there and then, having set it, had laid his tools down and walked away.

Iren wrote: Globe (Antwerp, 1602): site marked, name omitted at production.

She closed her notebook. She stood for a moment in the lobby with her hand on the cool brass and listened to the building. Somewhere on the floor below her, a clerk was weeping, very quietly, in the way people weep at their desks when they have not yet decided to admit that they are weeping. Somewhere on the floor above, two voices were arguing in low, measured tones, and although she could not make out the words she could tell, by the rhythm of them, that the argument was the kind that ends in nothing being decided. The Hall was waking up to itself. The Hall had no more idea what to do than the city did.

She went back upstairs.

* * *

In her own office she had a small private collection of documents that the chief archivist had, over the years, given her permission to keep at her own desk. There were perhaps thirty of them, all minor, all unimportant: a tax receipt from 1834, a playbill from a long-vanished theater, a single page from a dressmaker's order book of the 1880s with the names of six women and the colors of the dresses they had ordered for some forgotten ball. Iren had kept these things because she liked them. They were the small change of history. They were the proof that a city is not made of charters and treaties but of dresses and tax receipts, and she had wanted them within reach of her hand.

She took the dressmaker's page out of its sleeve and laid it flat on her desk.

The page read, at the top, in the dressmaker's careful copperplate: For the Spring Ball at the â€”â€” Assembly Rooms, this 14th of March, 1883.

The blank where the name should have been was as smooth as all the others. Beneath it, the six women's names were intact: Mrs. Pellan, Mrs. Auber, Miss Auber, Mrs. Vossi, Miss Vossi, Mrs. Karne. The colors of their dresses were intact: rose, dove, dove, sea-green, sea-green, black. The price each had paid was intact, in shillings and pence. Only the name of the assembly rooms â€” and therefore, at one remove, the name of the city in which the assembly rooms had stood â€” was gone.

Iren turned the page over. On the back, the dressmaker had written, in pencil, a small private note to herself: Mrs. Karne in mourning still. Send the bill in May. Do not press.

The note was untouched. The pencil was the pencil of an ordinary March afternoon in 1883. The dressmaker had finished writing the order, and turned the page, and written her note about the kindness owed to a widow, and then closed the book and gone home through streets whose name was already not there, and she had not noticed, and her customers had not noticed, and no one had ever noticed, in any year, in any document, in any cartouche of any map, that the name of the city was a hole the city had been built around.

Iren sat back in her chair.

She thought: this is older than this morning.

She thought: this is older than us.

* * *

A knock at her door. She said, "Yes," and the door opened, and a young man she had never seen before came in. He was tall and thin and had the kind of black hair that will not lie flat, and he was carrying a leather satchel, and he had on his face the bright, slightly desperate expression of a person who has been working all night and does not yet know it.

"Madam Vale?" he said.

"Iren is fine."

"Tomas Eyrn. The Daily â€”." He held out a hand. She took it. His palm was ink-stained and warm. "I'm sorry to come up unannounced. The man at the door said you were in. "What is your article about, Mr. Eyrn?"

"It is about a thing I cannot name." He smiled, briefly, at himself. "I had thought, in the small hours, that this was a clever way of putting it. I now find it is the only way of putting it. It is the literal truth. Madam Vale â€” Iren â€” may I sit down?"

"Please."

He sat. He opened his satchel. He took out a notebook of the same kind she used, and a pencil with a chewed end, and he laid these on his knee and looked at her with great seriousness.

"You are an archivist," he said. "If anyone in this city is in a position to know what has happened, it is you. So I will ask you straight. What has happened?"

Iren considered him. She considered the pencil and the notebook. She considered the article that would, in a few hours, be in the hands of every literate person within the city walls, and she considered the duty of an archivist, which is to say neither more nor less than the truth, even and especially when the truth is unwelcome.

"I do not know," she said.

He wrote that down.

"But I know," she said, "what has not happened. The name has not been erased. I want to be very precise about this, Mr. Eyrn, because I think the precision matters. If you write tomorrow that the name of our city was erased in the night, you will be wrong. If you write that the name vanished, you will be wrong. The name was never written. The name was never spoken. The name has never, in any document held by this Hall, in any inscription on any monument in any square, in any letter sent from this city to any other city, been recorded in any form. We have been a city without a name on paper since the day we were founded, and not one of us, until this morning, knew it."

Tomas Eyrn stopped writing. He looked up.

"That isn't possible," he said.

"That is what I have spent the last two hours discovering. I would have said the same thing this morning. I have read our founding charter, Mr. Eyrn. I have read it many times. I would have sworn to you on my mother's grave that the name of our city is written in red ink and gold leaf in four places on the first page. I have just looked at that page. There is no red ink. There is no gold leaf. The parchment has not been touched. "Then â€”" he began. He stopped. He started again. "Then how have we known what to call it?"

"We have spoken it to one another. We have taught it to our children. We have heard it from our parents. It has lived, Mr. Eyrn, in the air between us. It has lived in the breath. It has not lived on the page. And this morning something has gone out of the air."

"What?"

"I do not know."

He wrote that down too. He wrote slowly. When he had finished he looked at his notebook for a moment and then he looked up at her again, and Iren saw in his face the thing she had begun to see in every face she had passed in the corridors that morning â€” the look of a person who has been told, very late in life, that the floor of his house is not joined to the walls.

"Madam Vale," he said. "I have to ask you a question I do not want to ask. I have been thinking about it since the baker on my street told me, in tears, that he could not remember where he lived. Iren had been waiting, all morning, for someone to ask her that question. She had been asking it of herself in the corner of her mind where she put the questions she did not yet have permission to think about. She was glad it was Tomas Eyrn who had asked it, because Tomas Eyrn had a chewed pencil and ink-stained hands and had been awake all night writing the worst article of his life, and that was the kind of person to whom one could give an honest answer.

"I think," she said carefully, "that things do not happen to a city of this size at four o'clock in the morning by accident. I think that the absence I have been looking at all morning is too clean to be natural. Natural absences are ragged. They have edges. They have shadows. This absence has none. "By whom?"

"I do not know."

"For what reason?"

"I do not know." She paused. "But Mr. Eyrn â€” I will tell you the thing I have not yet told anyone, and you may put it in your article or not, as you choose. He set down his pencil. He did not write that.

"Why do you say so?"

"Because the parchment of the founding charter was prepared with the blanks already in place. Because the brass globe was cast with the site marked and the name omitted. Because the dressmaker in 1883 wrote her order around a hole and went home and slept the sleep of an honest woman. It would have taken our walls. It would have taken our river. It would have taken us. It has taken nothing but the name. That is not the work of an enemy, Mr. Eyrn. That is the work of a tenant who is very careful with his own house."

Tomas Eyrn looked at her for a long moment. Then he closed his notebook. He put the chewed pencil behind his ear. He stood up.

"Madam Vale," he said. "Thank you. I do not think I will write any of that. I do not think I will write very much at all today. I think I will go home and sit in my kitchen and think, and let the second edition print itself, and tomorrow I will come back, if you will allow it, and ask you again. May I?"

"Yes."

"Thank you." He paused at the door. "May I ask one last thing. Off the record. Not for the paper."

"Yes."

"Are you frightened?"

Iren considered the question with the seriousness it deserved. The key was cold against her chest under her collar. The two hundred volumes lay open on the tables of the central reading room two floors below. The dressmaker's order from 1883 lay flat on her desk, the widow Karne's mourning intact, the name of the assembly rooms a smooth absence above. The Hall hummed with the small uncertain sounds of a building in which everyone was pretending not to weep.

"No," she said. "Not yet. I think I will be, before the day is out. But I am not, yet. I am â€” interested, Mr. Eyrn. I am very interested. I think we have lived our whole lives in a house with a room in it we did not know was there, and the door has just opened, and I want to look inside before I am frightened.
